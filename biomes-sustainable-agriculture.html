Stage 5 Mandatory · Focus Area 2 · Physical and Human Geography

Biomes and Sustainable Agriculture

How do we feed the world without destroying the systems that make food production possible?

← Back to Stage 5 Mandatory
"Every meal you have ever eaten came from a biome. Most people have never thought about which one - or what it costs to keep that biome productive."
Biomes and Sustainable Agriculture examines the relationship between the world's major ecological zones and the agricultural systems that depend on them. Students investigate how biomes function, why they are under increasing pressure, and what sustainable agriculture looks like in practice. This unit sits at the intersection of physical and human geography - the natural world and the choices humans make about how to use it.

Unit Overview

A biome is a large-scale ecological zone defined by its climate, vegetation and wildlife. Tropical rainforests, grasslands, deserts, temperate forests and tundra are all biomes - and each one supports a different kind of agricultural system. Understanding the connection between biome and agriculture is central to this unit.

Students examine how human agricultural activity is altering the biomes it depends on - through deforestation, overgrazing, salinisation and pollution - and investigate what more sustainable approaches look like. Australia, as one of the world's major food exporters, provides a compelling local context throughout.

Inquiry Questions
These are the driving questions for this unit, based on the NESA inquiry approach.
What are the characteristics of biomes and how are they distributed across the Earth?
How do biomes support agricultural systems and food production?
What are the impacts of agricultural practices on biomes?
How can agricultural practices become more sustainable?
Key Ideas
What is a biome?
A biome is a large ecological region defined by its dominant climate and vegetation. The major biomes include tropical rainforest, tropical grassland (savanna), desert, Mediterranean, temperate grassland, temperate forest, boreal forest (taiga) and tundra. Each has distinctive characteristics that determine what can be grown there.
Biome distribution
Biomes are not randomly distributed - they follow patterns determined by latitude, altitude, ocean currents and prevailing winds. Australia contains several major biomes within its borders, from tropical rainforest in Queensland to desert in the centre to temperate forest in the south-east and south-west.
Agricultural systems
Different biomes support different types of farming. Tropical regions suit cash crops like sugar and coffee. Temperate grasslands support cattle and grain farming. Desert and semi-arid areas require irrigation. Understanding the match between biome and agriculture explains why farming looks different in different parts of the world.
Pressure on biomes
Agricultural expansion is the leading driver of deforestation globally. Overgrazing degrades grasslands. Irrigation leads to salinisation of soils. Fertiliser runoff pollutes waterways. Climate change is shifting the boundaries of biomes, making some areas less suitable for their existing agricultural systems.
Sustainable agriculture
Sustainable agriculture aims to produce food in ways that protect the long-term health of the biome it depends on. This includes practices such as crop rotation, reduced tillage, integrated pest management, agroforestry and the use of drought-resistant crop varieties. It also involves policy decisions about land use and water allocation.
Food security
Food security exists when all people have reliable access to sufficient, safe and nutritious food. It is threatened by biome degradation, climate change, population growth, conflict and inequality. Understanding the geography of food security - who has it, who does not, and why - is central to this unit.
Suggested Lesson Sequence
Approximately 6-8 weeks. Adapt to suit your class and timetable.
Teacher note: This unit combines physical geography (biome characteristics) with human geography (agricultural systems). If the physical content feels unfamiliar, start with Australia - students can connect to biomes they have actually experienced, and Australian examples are well documented and freely available.
LessonTitleWhat students doKey resources and activities
1What is a biome?Students are introduced to the concept of a biome and learn to distinguish biomes from ecosystems and habitats. They map the world's major biomes and identify Australia's biome regions.World biome map activity. Students shade and label a blank world map. Discussion: which biome do we live in? Australian Biomes map from IBRA (Interim Biogeographic Regionalisation for Australia).
2Biome characteristicsStudents investigate the climate, vegetation and wildlife of four major biomes using data and photographs. They construct climate graphs and annotate biome diagrams.Climate data for tropical rainforest, temperate grassland, desert and boreal forest. Students create a comparison table. Photographs for visual analysis.
3Agriculture and biomesStudents examine which agricultural systems operate in which biomes and why. They investigate the relationship between climate, soil type and what can be farmed profitably.World agricultural land use map. Case studies: wheat farming in the Australian wheatbelt (temperate grassland), sugar cane in Queensland (tropical), cattle grazing in northern Australia (savanna).
4Feeding a growing worldStudents investigate global food security - where people go hungry and why. They examine the difference between food availability and food access, and map hunger against agricultural production.FAO Global Hunger Index data. World food production vs. food insecurity maps. Discussion: why do people go hungry in countries that export food?
5How agriculture damages biomesStudents investigate the environmental impacts of modern agricultural practices - deforestation, soil degradation, water use, pollution - and examine a specific case in depth.Case study: land clearing in Queensland - rates, drivers and consequences for the Great Barrier Reef. Or: the expansion of soy farming into the Amazon. Students annotate cause-and-effect diagrams.
6Sustainable agriculture in practiceStudents examine a range of sustainable agricultural approaches and evaluate their effectiveness. They consider barriers to adoption as well as benefits.Case studies: regenerative grazing in Australia (Gabe Brown method), agroforestry in South-East Asia, precision agriculture using satellite data. Students evaluate: which approach is most realistic for Australian farmers?
7Climate change and biomesStudents investigate how climate change is altering biome boundaries and threatening agricultural systems. They examine projected changes for Australia specifically.CSIRO climate projections for Australian agriculture. Case study: the effect of drought intensification on the Murray-Darling Basin. Students use data to construct arguments about future food security.
8Responses and solutionsStudents evaluate strategies at different scales - individual farmer, national policy, international agreement - for making agriculture more sustainable within biomes.Examples: the Land Restoration Fund (Qld), carbon farming, the UN Decade on Ecosystem Restoration. Students rank strategies and justify their ranking using geographical evidence.
9-10Assessment taskStudents complete their response. See Assessment section below.Scaffold with a planning template: biome chosen, agricultural system examined, sustainability issue identified, strategy evaluated.
Case Study Suggestions
Australian biome and agriculture
The Murray-Darling Basin - irrigation agriculture and water conflict. The Great Barrier Reef catchment - land clearing and water quality. The wheatbelt of WA - dryland salinity. Northern Australia - cattle grazing in tropical savanna.
Global deforestation
Amazon deforestation for soy and cattle - Brazil. Palm oil expansion in Borneo - destruction of tropical rainforest. The Cerrado (Brazilian savanna) - one of the world's most threatened biomes.
Sustainable agriculture examples
Regenerative grazing - Australia and USA. Agroforestry - Sahel region, West Africa. Vertical farming - Singapore and Japan. Organic farming - Europe. Indigenous land management - fire-stick farming in Australia.
Food security case studies
The Sahel famine belt - chronic food insecurity. South Sudan - conflict and food crisis. India - the paradox of food production and hunger. Australia - exporter of food but pockets of domestic food insecurity.
Assessment Ideas
Option A
Geographical Report
Students investigate one biome, the agricultural system it supports, the pressures it faces, and one strategy for making that system more sustainable. Written report with maps and data, 600-800 words.
Option B
Extended Response
Stimulus-based response: students analyse a photograph or data set showing agricultural impact on a biome, then evaluate the effectiveness of a named management strategy.
Option C
Farmer's Dilemma
Students take the role of a farmer in a given biome facing pressure to produce more food. They research their options and present a justified decision about how to farm more sustainably. Develops empathy and real-world application.
Option D
Infographic or Visual Report
Students create a one-page infographic or visual report on one agricultural system and its impact on a biome. Must include a map, at least one graph, and a clear conclusion. Good for visual learners and students who struggle with extended writing.
Assessment tip: Push students beyond description. "The Amazon is being deforested" is description. "Soy farming in the Amazon is profitable in the short term but threatens the long-term agricultural viability of the region because..." is geographical analysis. Teach students to always follow a claim with because.
