Stage 5 Mandatory · Focus Area 1 · Human Geography

Changing Places

Why do places change? Who decides? And what happens to the people already there?

← Back to Stage 5 Mandatory
"Your suburb looked completely different 30 years ago. In another 30 years, it will look different again. Geography asks who decides what it becomes - and who pays the price."
Changing Places explores how and why places change over time - through population movement, urban growth, economic shifts, and the decisions of governments, developers and communities. Students learn that places are not fixed. They are constantly being made and remade by human choices and physical processes, and that understanding this change is central to thinking geographically.

Unit Overview

This unit is built around one central idea: places are not permanent. Every suburb, city, rural town and coastline you can name has changed - and is still changing. Some of that change is driven by physical geography. Much of it is driven by human decisions: where to build, who to welcome, which industries to attract, which to abandon.

Students investigate change at different scales - from a single street to a global city - and develop the geographical thinking skills to explain why change happens, who benefits, and who is left behind. The unit connects directly to Stage 6 Geography, where Changing Places is studied in greater depth.

Inquiry Questions
NESA structures Stage 5 Geography around inquiry questions. These are the driving questions for this unit.
How do places, people and environments change?
Why are places changing at different rates and in different ways?
What are the consequences of change for places, people and environments?
How can the sustainability of places be improved?
Key Ideas
Students need to understand these core geographical ideas across the unit.
Push and pull factors
People move to and from places for reasons that can be organised as push factors (things that drive people away - poverty, conflict, lack of opportunity) and pull factors (things that attract people - jobs, safety, services). Both operate at local and global scales.
Urbanisation
More than half the world's population now lives in cities, and that proportion is growing. Urbanisation brings opportunities but also challenges - congestion, inequality, pressure on services and environmental degradation.
Gentrification
When wealthier residents move into lower-income urban areas, property values rise and the character of the neighbourhood changes. Long-term residents can be priced out. Gentrification is contested - some see it as renewal, others as displacement.
Rural decline and renewal
Many rural communities in Australia face population loss as young people move to cities. Some are finding new futures through tourism, renewable energy or remote working. Understanding why this happens - and what can be done - is central to this unit.
Interconnection between places
No place changes in isolation. A new train line affects suburbs along the route. A drought in regional NSW affects food prices in cities. Global trade patterns determine whether a rural town thrives or empties. Change in one place ripples through others.
Sustainability of places
Geographers do not only describe change - they evaluate it. Is this change making a place more or less liveable? More or less fair? Sustainability asks whether the needs of the future are being protected alongside the needs of today.
Suggested Lesson Sequence
This sequence is a guide - adapt it to suit your class and available time. The unit works well over approximately 6-8 weeks.
Teacher note: You do not need to be a geography specialist to teach this unit well. The key is helping students ask good questions about the places they already know. Start with somewhere local - your suburb, your school's neighbourhood - and build outward from there.
LessonTitleWhat students doKey resources and activities
1What is a place?Students explore the difference between a location (coordinates on a map) and a place (somewhere with meaning, character and community). They reflect on what makes their local area distinct.Photo analysis of local streetscapes - now vs. historical. Mental mapping activity. Discussion: "What would make your suburb unrecognisable?"
2How do places change?Students investigate the drivers of change - population growth, migration, economic shifts, environmental pressures, government policy and technology. They sort examples into categories.Case study carousel: urban growth in Western Sydney, rural decline in Broken Hill, coastal erosion at Collaroy. Students annotate maps showing change over time.
3Push and pull factorsStudents examine why people move between places, using Australian and global examples. They create push and pull factor diagrams.ABS Census data on population movement. Student interviews with family about migration stories. Comparison: rural to urban migration in Australia vs. a developing-world example.
4Urbanisation - global and localStudents map global urbanisation trends and examine what rapid city growth means for services, environment and quality of life.UN World Cities Data. Google Earth timelapse of urban growth. Case study: Dharavi, Mumbai - informal settlement and planned redevelopment. Discussion of rights and belonging.
5Gentrification - who benefits?Students investigate gentrification as a process, exploring both the benefits and the costs. They analyse a local Sydney example and evaluate competing perspectives.Case study: Redfern or Surry Hills - historical vs. current character. Stakeholder activity: developer, long-term resident, council planner, social housing tenant. Structured debate.
6Rural change in AustraliaStudents explore why some rural areas are declining and others are reinventing themselves. They examine the geographical, economic and social factors involved.Case studies: Bourke, NSW (decline) vs. Armidale (university town renewal) vs. Byron Bay (sea-change growth). Students propose strategies for a struggling rural town.
7Interconnection and consequencesStudents analyse how change in one place creates change in others - through supply chains, migration, infrastructure and environmental flows.Scenario: a major employer closes a regional factory. Students trace effects outward - to workers, local businesses, schools, nearby cities. Class discussion on scale.
8Making places more sustainableStudents evaluate strategies used to make changing places more sustainable - for people, communities and environments.Case study: Green Square, Sydney, or Lochiel Park, Adelaide. Students use a PEEL paragraph structure to evaluate one strategy's effectiveness. Connects to SDG 11.
9-10Assessment taskStudents complete their response. See Assessment section below.Scaffold with a planning template: inquiry question, case study chosen, evidence to use, structure of response.

Mapping and aerial photos
Mapping change over time, annotating aerial photographs, interpreting population distribution maps.

Inquiry skills
Framing questions
Framing a geographical question, selecting evidence, evaluating multiple perspectives, constructing an argument.
Visual analysis
Photographs and satellite images
Interpreting photographs and satellite images for evidence of change, comparing historical and current images.
Source evaluation
Reliability and bias
Assessing the reliability of sources, identifying perspective and bias in media and planning documents.
Extended writing
PEEL paragraphs
Constructing PEEL paragraphs, structuring a geographical argument, using evidence to support evaluative claims.
Case Study Suggestions
Choose case studies at different scales. NESA expects students to study change at local, national and global levels.
Teacher note: Always include at least one Australian example and one international example. Students engage more deeply when one case study feels personal - your local area, a place someone in the class has lived, or somewhere in the news.
Local and Australian urban
Western Sydney growth corridor (Penrith, Parramatta, Liverpool). Redfern or Surry Hills gentrification. Green Square urban renewal. Port Adelaide transformation. Fitzroy or Collingwood in Melbourne.
Australian rural
Broken Hill - declining mining town. Bourke, NSW - remote rural decline. Armidale - regional city sustained by a university. Byron Bay - coastal town experiencing sea-change population growth.
Global urban - developing world
Dharavi, Mumbai - rapid urbanisation and informal settlements. Lagos, Nigeria - one of the fastest-growing cities on Earth. Shenzhen, China - planned city transformation from fishing village to global tech hub.
Global urban - developed world
Detroit, USA - deindustrialisation and urban shrinkage. Bilbao, Spain - cultural regeneration after industrial collapse. Singapore - planned, highly managed urban growth as a model city-state.
Assessment Ideas
Option A
Geographical Inquiry Report
Students choose a place that has changed significantly and investigate why, what the consequences are, and how sustainable the change is. Written report, 600-800 words, with maps and data included.
Option B
Extended Response
Students respond to a stimulus (photograph, map, article) with an extended written answer. Two parts: describe the change shown, then evaluate the consequences for people and environments.
Option C
Multimodal Presentation
Students create a short presentation examining a changing place of their choice. Must include at least one map, one data source and a clear geographical argument.
Option D
Stakeholder Letter
Students write from the perspective of a stakeholder affected by change in a real place - a long-term resident facing displacement, or a farmer dealing with rural decline. Must use geographical evidence.
Assessment tip: Whichever task you use, give students a clear scaffold. The key geographical move is always the same: describe what is happening, explain why, and evaluate the consequences. Explicitly teach students to link evidence to their claims rather than simply listing facts.
