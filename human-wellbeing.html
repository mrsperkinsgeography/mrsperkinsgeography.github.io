Stage 5 Mandatory · Focus Area 4 · Human Geography

Human Wellbeing

Why is where you are born still the single biggest factor in how long and how well you will live?

← Back to Stage 5 Mandatory
"A child born in Australia today can expect to live to 83. A child born in Sierra Leone can expect to live to 54. Both are born into the same world. Geography asks why."
Human Wellbeing examines what it means to live well - and why that varies so dramatically from one place to another. Students investigate how geographers measure wellbeing, what drives inequality within and between countries, and what responses - from international aid to local community action - are attempting to close the gap. This unit connects directly to the UN Sustainable Development Goals and gives students a framework for thinking about global justice.

Unit Overview

Wellbeing is more than wealth. Geographers measure human wellbeing through a range of indicators - life expectancy, education, access to clean water, gender equality, political freedom - and examine how these vary across the world and within individual countries. The spatial patterns that emerge from this data tell a powerful story about inequality, opportunity and the decisions that shape people's lives.

Students examine what drives differences in wellbeing, investigate responses at different scales, and evaluate how effective those responses are. Australia's own wellbeing inequalities - including the gap between Indigenous and non-Indigenous Australians - provide an important and honest domestic context alongside global comparisons.

Inquiry Questions
What is human wellbeing and how is it measured?
Why does human wellbeing vary between and within countries?
What are the consequences of differences in wellbeing?
How can human wellbeing be improved and for whom?
Key Ideas
Defining wellbeing
Wellbeing is a broad concept that goes beyond income. It includes physical health, mental health, education, access to services, safety, environmental quality and social connection. How we define and measure it shapes which problems we see and which we miss.
Measuring wellbeing
Key indicators include the Human Development Index (HDI), which combines life expectancy, education and income. Other measures include the Multidimensional Poverty Index, the Gender Inequality Index and the Happy Planet Index. Each captures different aspects - and each has limitations. Students need to use multiple indicators and think critically about what each one reveals.
Global patterns of inequality
Human wellbeing is not randomly distributed - it follows geographical patterns linked to history (colonialism, conflict), physical geography (landlocked nations, climate), economic systems (trade terms, debt) and governance. Mapping these patterns is a fundamental skill in this unit.
Inequality within countries
Even wealthy countries have significant internal wellbeing inequalities. In Australia, Indigenous Australians experience significantly lower life expectancy, higher rates of chronic disease, lower education levels and higher incarceration rates than non-Indigenous Australians. Understanding this domestic inequality is essential and must be handled with care and respect.
Causes of low wellbeing
Low wellbeing has interconnected causes: poverty limits access to healthcare and education; poor health reduces economic productivity; limited education restricts opportunity; conflict destroys infrastructure; environmental degradation reduces agricultural capacity. These causes reinforce each other in cycles that are difficult to break.
Responses to inequality
Responses operate at different scales: international aid, debt relief and trade reform at the global level; government spending and policy at the national level; community organisations and local initiatives at the local level. Evaluating which responses are most effective - and why some fail - is a core task of this unit.
Suggested Lesson Sequence
Approximately 6-8 weeks. This unit has significant potential for student discussion and debate - build in time for that alongside the data and writing work.
Teacher note: This unit deals with inequality, poverty and disadvantage - topics that may be personally relevant to some students. Approach with sensitivity. Focus on structural causes rather than individual blame, and make space for students to ask honest questions about why the world is this way and whether it has to be.
LessonTitleWhat students doKey resources and activities
1What is wellbeing?Students explore different definitions of wellbeing - economic, social, environmental, cultural. They reflect on what makes a good life and where geography enters the picture.Discussion activity: students rank what matters most in life. Introduction to the difference between GDP per capita and the HDI. Students examine a country that scores differently on each.
2Measuring wellbeingStudents investigate the main indicators used to measure wellbeing and their limitations. They practise reading and interpreting data tables and world maps of indicators.UNDP Human Development Report data. Students map HDI by country and identify patterns. Critical thinking: what does HDI miss? Introduction to alternative measures.
3Global patternsStudents analyse the spatial distribution of wellbeing globally - which regions score highest and lowest, and what geographical factors help explain the pattern.World maps of life expectancy, literacy, income and access to clean water. Students write a paragraph describing and beginning to explain global patterns. Introduction to the concept of the Global North and Global South.
4Why does wellbeing vary?Students investigate the causes of wellbeing inequality at the global scale - historical, geographical, economic and political. They examine how these causes connect and reinforce each other.Case study: compare Norway (very high HDI) with Niger (low HDI). What explains the difference? Students create a cause-and-effect web. Discussion: is low wellbeing a natural outcome or a result of decisions?
5Inequality within AustraliaStudents examine wellbeing inequalities within Australia - between states, between urban and rural areas, and between Indigenous and non-Indigenous Australians.Closing the Gap data. AIHW reports on Indigenous health. ABS data on regional wellbeing. Students compare life expectancy, education and employment data. Discussion: what is Australia's responsibility to address these gaps?
6Country case study in depthStudents investigate one country with low wellbeing in depth - its indicators, the causes of its situation, and the challenges it faces in improving wellbeing for its people.Suggested case studies: Papua New Guinea (close to Australia, complex relationship), Bangladesh (rapid improvement story), South Sudan (conflict and aid). Students create a detailed country profile.
7Responses to inequalityStudents examine a range of responses to wellbeing inequality - international aid, trade reform, microfinance, education programs, community health workers - and evaluate their effectiveness.Case study: the work of Australian Aid (DFAT) in the Pacific. The role of the UN and WHO. Microfinance examples - Grameen Bank. Students evaluate: which type of intervention has the most evidence behind it?
8The SDGs and human wellbeingStudents connect the content of this unit to the UN Sustainable Development Goals, examining how the SDGs address wellbeing inequality and how progress is being measured.UNDP SDG progress tracker. Focus on SDG 1 (No Poverty), SDG 3 (Good Health), SDG 4 (Quality Education), SDG 10 (Reduced Inequalities). Students assess: are we on track?
9-10Assessment taskStudents complete their response. See Assessment section below.Scaffold: country chosen, wellbeing indicators examined, causes of inequality identified, response evaluated, conclusion about what matters most.
Case Study Suggestions
Countries with improving wellbeing
Bangladesh - rapid gains in life expectancy and gender equality despite low income. Rwanda - post-genocide recovery and health system investment. China - dramatic poverty reduction over 40 years. These cases illustrate that change is possible.
Countries with persistent low wellbeing
Niger, Mali, Chad - persistent poverty and climate vulnerability in the Sahel. Papua New Guinea - near neighbour to Australia with very low HDI. South Sudan - conflict and aid dependency. These cases examine why change is hard.
Australian domestic inequality
The Closing the Gap framework - targets, progress and gaps. Remote community wellbeing in the Northern Territory. Regional vs. urban wellbeing in NSW. The geography of disadvantage in Australian cities.
Aid and development programs
Australian Aid programs in the Pacific - water, health and education. UNICEF child health programs in sub-Saharan Africa. The Grameen Bank microfinance model in Bangladesh. Fair trade and its effect on farmer incomes.
Assessment Ideas
Option A
Country Report
Students choose a country with low or improving wellbeing, investigate its wellbeing indicators and causes, and evaluate one strategy being used to improve it. Written report with data and maps, 600-800 words.
Option B
Comparative Response
Students compare two countries at different stages of development using wellbeing data, explain the geographical factors that account for the difference, and evaluate what each country is doing to improve its situation.
Option C
Aid Agency Brief
Students take the role of an analyst at an aid organisation and write a brief recommending where funding should be directed and why. Must use HDI data, identify root causes, and justify the recommendation geographically.
Option D
Closing the Gap Analysis
Students investigate the Australian Government's Closing the Gap framework - what it measures, what progress has been made, and what the data shows about remaining gaps. Develops students' ability to read official data critically.
Assessment tip: Encourage students to look for geographical explanations rather than cultural or racial ones. "Niger is poor because of its climate, landlocked geography, colonial history and terms of trade" is a geographical argument. "Niger is poor because of its people" is not - and it is wrong. Help students develop the habit of asking "what structural factors explain this?"
