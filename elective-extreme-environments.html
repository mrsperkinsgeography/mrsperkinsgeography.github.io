Stage 5 Elective 2027 - Physical Geography

️ Extreme Environments

Starting 2027 - Stage 5 Geography Elective

← Back to all elective units
Being developed Full lesson content, case studies and resources for this unit will appear here as they are built. Check back soon.
"Could you survive in the Sahara? Why do people choose to live where the Earth is trying to kill them?"
Extreme environments are places where physical conditions make human survival genuinely difficult. Understanding how these environments work - and how people and ecosystems adapt - is one of the most compelling areas of physical geography.

Prospective unit outline
Please note: This is a working plan based on the NESA sample unit. Topics, order and timing may change as the unit is developed. More detail will be added to each week as lessons are built.
Shared content
What is an extreme environment?
Not every dangerous place qualifies. This week draws a clear line between extreme environments and extreme events - and starts building the global picture.
Where on Earth are they?
Mapping the world's most hostile places - deserts, polar regions, high-altitude zones and geothermal areas. Why are they where they are?
What makes them so hard to survive?
Temperature, chemical conditions, oxygen, terrain. Reading real data from some of Earth's most unforgiving places.
Sun, tilt and seasons
Why does the angle of sunlight determine whether a place is ice or desert? Earth's tilt, latitude and the science behind extreme seasonal variation.
Going up: the role of altitude
Every 1000 metres of elevation costs you about 6 degrees. This week explores how altitude, pressure and thin air push environments to their limits.
Getting more extreme
Climate change is not coming - in many of these places, it is already here. Glaciers retreating, deserts expanding, lakes dying. The evidence is striking.
Earth at work: geomorphic and geophysical processes
Volcanoes, tectonics, glaciation and erosion. How the planet's internal and surface processes sculpt the most dramatic landscapes on Earth.
Soil, terrain and chemical conditions
Frozen ground, salt flats, acidic pools. This week examines what the ground itself does to the possibility of life - and what life does in response.
When factors collide
Extremes rarely come alone. This week brings together physical, chemical and climatic factors - and asks: what happens when all three hit at once? Students also confirm their investigation choice.
Investigation into one of these
You choose one extreme environment and investigate it in depth. Same framework, your place. More detail on each option will be added here as the unit develops.
The Danakil Depression, Ethiopia
One of the hottest, lowest and most chemically hostile places on Earth - and people have lived there for centuries.
The Himalayas, Nepal
Where altitude kills, glaciers are retreating and Everest has a traffic problem. The Sherpa people have called it home for generations.
The Simpson Desert, Australia
The closest to home. One of the driest places on the continent, shaped by Aboriginal knowledge going back tens of thousands of years.
Antarctica
The coldest, windiest, most isolated continent. No one owns it. Everyone is watching it melt.
The Dead Sea region, Jordan
The lowest point on Earth - and shrinking fast. Salt, potash mining, Bedouin tradition and a water crisis that crosses borders.
Features and characteristics of your environment
What makes your chosen place extreme? Physical features, climate, geomorphic processes. First impressions become geographical evidence.
How life survives here
Microbes, plants, animals and humans have all found ways in. This week examines the adaptations - structural, functional and behavioural - that make survival possible.
Technology and human interaction
From salt-cutting tools to GPS and drones. How has technology changed what humans can do in extreme environments - and what they extract from them?
Indigenous knowledge and connection to place
Long before scientists arrived, Indigenous peoples had mapped these environments in extraordinary detail. What do their knowledge systems reveal - and what can the rest of the world learn?
Contemporary issues
Tourism, mining, climate change, conservation. Who benefits from your environment - and who bears the cost? Stakeholder perspectives and a look at what sustainable management could mean.
