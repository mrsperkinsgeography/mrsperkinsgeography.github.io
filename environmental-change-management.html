Stage 5 Mandatory · Focus Area 3 · Environmental Geography

Environmental Change and Management

When an environment is under pressure, who is responsible for managing it - and how much difference can management actually make?

← Back to Stage 5 Mandatory
"The Great Barrier Reef is the largest living structure on Earth. It can be seen from space. And it is dying. Geography asks not just why - but what, if anything, can actually be done about it."
Environmental Change and Management examines how environments are altered - through both natural processes and human activity - and what responses are available when that change becomes a problem. Students investigate specific environments under pressure, evaluate the management strategies being applied, and develop the geographical skills to assess whether those strategies are working. This unit is highly relevant: the environments students study are in the news, and the decisions being made about them will shape Australia and the world for generations.

Unit Overview

Environments change constantly - that is normal. What concerns geographers is when the rate or scale of change exceeds the capacity of ecosystems and communities to adapt. This unit examines how that happens, why it matters, and what management responses look like in practice.

Students investigate at least one environment undergoing significant change and evaluate the strategies being used to manage it. Australia provides exceptional case study material - the Great Barrier Reef, the Murray-Darling Basin, coastal erosion, bushfire-affected landscapes and urban heat islands are all well documented and deeply relevant to students' lives.

Inquiry Questions
How and why do environments change?
What are the causes and consequences of environmental change?
How do people and organisations respond to environmental change?
How effective are management strategies in improving environmental sustainability?
Key Ideas
Natural vs. human-induced change
Environments change through natural processes - volcanic eruptions, sea level fluctuations, natural fire cycles, drought - and through human activity. In most contemporary environmental problems, both are involved. Distinguishing between them, and understanding how they interact, is a core geographical skill.
Scale of change
Environmental change can be local (a polluted river reach), regional (coral bleaching on a reef), national (bushfire season extent) or global (rising sea levels). The scale at which change occurs determines what kind of management response is possible - and who has the authority to act.
Causes and consequences
Every environmental change has causes that can be traced geographically - land use decisions, industrial emissions, infrastructure development, agricultural runoff. And every change has consequences for ecosystems, biodiversity, communities and economies. Understanding these chains of cause and consequence is central to this unit.
Management approaches
Management strategies can be placed on a spectrum from mitigation (reducing the cause of the problem) to adaptation (adjusting to its effects). Some focus on protection, others on restoration, others on regulation. Evaluating which approach is most effective - and in whose interests - requires geographical thinking.
Stakeholders and conflict
Environmental management always involves competing interests. Farmers, tourism operators, scientists, Indigenous communities, governments and industry often disagree about what the problem is and what should be done about it. Understanding these competing perspectives is essential to evaluating management strategies.
Evaluating effectiveness
A management strategy is not automatically effective just because it exists. Geographers assess whether strategies are achieving their goals, who is benefiting, what limitations exist, and whether the strategy is sustainable long term. This evaluation is a key skill developed throughout this unit.
Suggested Lesson Sequence
Approximately 6-8 weeks. The unit works best when anchored to one or two environments investigated in depth, rather than many environments covered superficially.
Teacher note: The Great Barrier Reef is an excellent anchor case study - it is well documented, deeply Australian, constantly in the news, and connects physical and human geography naturally. The Murray-Darling Basin works equally well for inland schools. Choose an environment your students feel some connection to.
LessonTitleWhat students doKey resources and activities
1What is environmental change?Students distinguish between natural and human-induced environmental change, and examine examples at different scales. They begin building a case study on a chosen environment.Photo comparison: environments before and after change. Examples: coral bleaching, bushfire, coastal erosion, urban expansion over wetlands. Students begin an annotated case study template.
2How environments workStudents investigate the structure and function of their chosen environment - what makes it work, what makes it vulnerable, and what it provides for people and ecosystems.For Great Barrier Reef: coral biology, ecosystem services (tourism, fisheries, coastal protection). For Murray-Darling: catchment hydrology, dependent communities. Students create a systems diagram.
3Causes of changeStudents investigate the causes of environmental change in their chosen case study. They distinguish between direct and indirect causes and examine how human activity amplifies natural pressures.For Great Barrier Reef: ocean warming, ocean acidification, crown-of-thorns starfish, agricultural runoff, coastal development. Students create a cause-and-effect chain diagram.
4Consequences of changeStudents examine the consequences of environmental change - for ecosystems, for biodiversity, for dependent communities and for economies. They think about who is most affected and why.Data on coral cover decline. Economic value of reef tourism. Community impact on reef-dependent fishing communities. Indigenous connections to Country. Students evaluate: who loses most?
5Who has a stake?Students identify and analyse the perspectives of different stakeholders in the management of their chosen environment. They examine conflicting interests and contested evidence.Stakeholder cards: Great Barrier Reef Marine Park Authority, Queensland farmers, tourism operators, Indigenous ranger groups, mining companies, climate scientists. Structured discussion activity.
6Management strategiesStudents investigate the range of management strategies in place for their chosen environment - from international agreements to local community action - and classify them by type and scale.Reef 2050 Long-Term Sustainability Plan. Great Barrier Reef Marine Park zoning. Water licence reform in the Murray-Darling. Students create a management strategy summary table.
7How effective is the management?Students evaluate the effectiveness of one or two management strategies using evidence. They consider what is working, what is not, and what the barriers to better management are.Scientific reports on reef health trends. ACCC water market review data. Independent assessments of management outcomes. Students write an evaluated paragraph using PEEL structure.
8What should be done?Students synthesise their learning to make and justify a recommendation about what the most important management priority should be for their chosen environment.Students construct a reasoned recommendation, referencing causes, consequences and existing management. Class discussion: whose job is it to protect this environment - individuals, governments or corporations?
9-10Assessment taskStudents complete their assessment response. See Assessment section below.Scaffold with planning template: environment chosen, key change described, causes identified, consequences for people and ecosystems, strategy evaluated, recommendation made.
Case Study Suggestions
Australian aquatic environments
The Great Barrier Reef - coral bleaching, agricultural runoff, tourism pressure and management. The Murray-Darling Basin - water allocation, drought, salinity and governance conflict. Sydney's coastal beaches - erosion, stormwater and beach nourishment programs.
Australian terrestrial environments
Bushfire-affected landscapes - causes, ecological consequences and recovery management. The Daintree Rainforest - clearing history, World Heritage status and tourism management. Urban wetlands - loss, restoration and the role of local councils.
Global environments under pressure
The Amazon rainforest - deforestation rates, international pressure and Brazilian policy. The Aral Sea - one of the world's worst environmental disasters and attempted restoration. Pacific island environments - sea level rise, saltwater intrusion and community relocation decisions.
Climate-related change
Arctic sea ice - rate of loss, ecosystem consequences, geopolitical implications. Alpine environments - shrinking snowfields, ski industry adaptation, plant community changes. Mangrove dieback - northern Australia's 2015-16 mass mangrove death event.
Assessment Ideas
Option A
Environmental Management Report
Students investigate one environment undergoing change: describe what is changing, explain the causes, assess the consequences, and evaluate the effectiveness of one management strategy. Written report, 600-800 words, with maps and data.
Option B
Stimulus-Based Extended Response
Students respond to a set of stimuli (map, graph, photograph) about a named environment. Part A: describe the environmental change shown. Part B: evaluate the effectiveness of strategies used to manage it. Develops exam writing skills.
Option C
Management Recommendation Brief
Students write a short brief addressed to a decision-maker (environment minister, council, reef authority) recommending what the most urgent management priority should be and why. Must be supported with geographical evidence.
Option D
Documentary Analysis
Students watch a documentary on an environment under pressure, then write a critical geographical analysis: what does the documentary show? What does the data say? What is missing? What management does it recommend and how effective is that likely to be?
Assessment tip: The word "evaluate" is central to this unit. Teach students what it means: not just describing a strategy, but making a judgement about how effective it is and supporting that judgement with evidence. A strong response says "this strategy has been partially effective because..." rather than "this strategy is good."
